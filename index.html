1. What percentage of pregnancies in the U.S. are unplanned?
Answer: 50%. Researchers estimate that about half of all pregnancies are unplanned, even though 90 percent of women use some form of birth control. Because unplanned pregnancies are so common, many women drink before they know they are pregnant. That's why the CDC recommends that women avoid alcohol if they are sexually active and not using effective contraception.

2. At what point during pregnancy is the fetus affected by alcohol consumption?
Answer: Any point during pregnancy. Unlike some other teratogens, alcohol may affect a fetus at any time during gestation (pregnancy). There's no way to know whether, or to what extent, a fetus may be affected by alcohol exposure.

4. When can a person start to demonstrate symptoms of FASD?
Answer: All of the above. An infant may be born with obvious physical symptoms of FASD, but other symptoms may not appear until early childhood or adolescence. Often FASD may be misdiagnosed as ADHD or autism. Experts believe that there may be a large number of adults living with undiagnosed FASDs.

5. Why are the effects of alcohol different for every pregnancy?
Answer: All of the above. Two fetuses may be exposed to the same amount and type of alcohol at the same time during pregnancy and experience very different effects. Experts believe that genetics play a role in determining the extent to which a fetus is affected by alcohol in the womb. There is currently no way to predict how an individual fetus will be affected.

6. What can you do if you or a person you care about is exhibiting signs of FASD?
Answer: Talk to your doctor about your concerns and request a referral for assessment by an FASD diagnostic team. Come to your appointment equipped with questions to ask your doctor. Your primary care physician may not be able to make a diagnosis, but they can refer you for further assessment by diagnostic experts.
